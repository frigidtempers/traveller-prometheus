## Overview

The **Bogleg** is a biological xenotype originally engineered for life on polluted wetlands, toxic marshes, and chemically hazardous environments. Distinguished by their amphibian features, resilient physiology, and instinctive aptitude for commerce, Boglegs have become a familiar sight wherever trade extends beyond established civilization.

Although originally developed for environmental adaptation, Boglegs are better known for their role as merchants, brokers, caravan operators, and independent traders. Their willingness to travel between isolated settlements has made them one of the most recognizable commercial xenotypes throughout human civilization.

---

## Biology

Boglegs possess a gene package incorporating traits derived from amphibians and venom-resistant reptiles. Their broad feet, moisture-retaining skin, and enhanced tolerance to environmental toxins allow them to operate effectively in wetlands and contaminated regions where baseliners often struggle.

Their appearance is frequently described as unsettling by other human populations. Bulging eyes, mottled skin, and exaggerated facial features are common characteristics, though considerable variation exists between individuals.

---

## Society

Commerce occupies a central role in Bogleg culture. Whether employed by Corporate Houses, operating independent caravans, or negotiating trade between isolated settlements, Boglegs have developed a widespread reputation for bargaining, logistics, and long-distance commerce.

Their appearance often leads to unfair prejudice among other xenotypes. Many Boglegs learn to leverage these assumptions to their advantage, developing reputations as shrewd negotiators capable of recognizing value where others cannot.

---

## Presence on Prometheus

Boglegs are uncommon permanent residents of Prometheus but are regularly encountered through inter-settlement trade and commercial expeditions.

Most Boglegs encountered on the planet work as independent merchants, caravan operators, brokers, or representatives of larger commercial organizations. Their willingness to travel between distant settlements often makes them valuable sources of goods, information, and news from beyond the local region.
